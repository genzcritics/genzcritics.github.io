Struggling against Sexism

In Stationed, Ruthie is trapped in a place where she is neither respected nor appreciated

by Elizabeth Kim on August 28, 2016

Stationed painstakingly details the ways in which Ruthie, an Israeli soldier, is disadvantaged by her gender. She can’t address her fellow soldiers in the military without hearing comments about her appearance; she can’t enter a dining room of the compound during a religious ceremony; and she is prevented from accessing the laundry machine by an unsympathetic male captain, though she desperately needs to wash her undergarments. At the end of the film, she hangs her hand-washed underwear up in the courtyard in plain sight, perhaps as a small act of defiance. Privacy, access to a single-sex washroom, freedom to enter public areas—all these basic rights are denied to Ruthie because she is a woman. The film’s dutiful documentation of the sexism demonstrated towards Ruthie feels restricting and tedious at times. But, perhaps this is filmmaker Nitzan Zifrut’s intention: to force the audience relate to Ruthie’s sense of feeling trapped in a place where she is neither respected nor appreciated. As the sole female soldier at her post, Ruthie should be a figure of heroism and inspiration to other women. Certainly, the idea of the rare female soldier brings to mind a swashbuckling, Joan of Arc-type heroine holding her own on a battlefield amidst a sea of men. The reality of Ruthie’s situation, however, is that she is suppressed and powerless, “stationed” at a place where she is forced to suppress her femininity.
